Each Malaysian voter can only casts one vote to elect one Member of Parliament (MP) but under the current electoral system, not every vote has the same power. For example, in a thinly-populated constituency like Putrajaya, the 17,925 voters there vote to elect one MP while in a over-crowded constituency like Subang, 130,211 voters - seven times the number of voters in Putrajaya - also elect one MP.

In other words, one Putrajaya vote is equal to seven Subang vote. Subang voters are under-represented while Putrajaya voter is over-represented. This malpractice is called malapportionment, a violation of the “one person, one vote, one value” principle in a democratic political system.

Let's look deeper into malapportionment at the national level.

There are
222
parliamentary seats in the whole country.
Each of them is represented by one line. The length of the line represents the number of voters in that seat.
The voter size of seats range from

BN won most of the seats with fewer voters. The average size of a BN seat is
48,273.
The rest were won by the opposition. The average size of an opposition seat is
79,921.
If we only look at rural seats, the voter size difference among them is still big.
It is even bigger among urban seats.
Malapportionment is part of the reason why in the 2013 general election, BN retained its power by winning
60%
of parliamentary seats with a mere
47%
of total votes while the opposition coalition could only win
40%
of seats with
51%
of votes.
